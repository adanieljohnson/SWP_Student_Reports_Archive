Title: Growth in Manduca Sexta when exposed to increased levels of JH

Abstract:
The purpose of this experiment was to study the effects of juvenile hormone on the growth of the Manduca Sexta. We hypothesized that if juvenile hormone, which is already produced within the caterpillar, was added to the food that the caterpillar, it would continue to grow past its pupation stage and eventually reach a size that its body could no longer support. We tested this hypothesis by splitting eight caterpillars into two groups of four. One group was fed normal food while the other was fed normal food with and 2% of juvenile hormone mixed in. We recorded the weight and refed that caterpillars every day. After about a week and a half of testing we came to the conclusion that our hypothesis was correct because the treated group continued to grow when the controll group reached its pupation stage.

Introduction:
Insects need both JH and 20E to survive and develop. 20E is a hormone that is produced by the prothoracic glands and works together with JH which is produced with the corpus alata to controll the growth cycle of Manduca Sexta (Tobacco Hornworm). This is done through a process called the commitment pulse. The commitment pulse occurs 5th larval instar where JH level drop and 20E levels spike. Since the role of juvenile hormone (JH) in regulating the timing and nature of insect molts is well-established, manipulating the concentration will alter the life cycle of the caterpillar. Increasing evidence suggests that JH is also involved in regulating final insect size. (Mirth, 2014). Since there are studies that show that altering levels of JH will have an effects on the growth of insects, my lab group decided that we should test what increasing the levels of JH in the Tobacco Hornworm to see if it would affect the growth of the caterpillar and if so, how it would affect the growth. Based on the information we learned in class if the concentration of 20E and JH are altered, the process of growth, molting, and metamorphosis are impacted in a way that does not allow the tobacco hornworm to pupate. Failure to pupate will cause the Tobacco Hornworm to continue to produce high levels of JH and 20E until the caterpillar grows to a point where the body can no longer support itself and death will eventually occur. We hypothesis that if we add 2% of JH hormone to 10 grams of food specially made to meet the nutritional needs of the tobacco hornworm, it will continue to grow but never reach a pupation stage.

Materials and Method:
For this experiment we took eight caterpillars and split them evenly into two groups. one was our controll group which was fed ten grams of the pre made tobacco horn worm food that had no added hormones. The other groups was also fed ten grams of the tobacco hornworm food but they had an added 2 %of JH hormone. This is equivalent to about 12 drops of the hormone solution in a 40 gram sample of the food. The JH hormone acts as a non-lethal pesticide. It will allow the caterpillar to grow but never pupate. Eventually they will grow to a size that there organs will no longer be able to support (Johnson, 2016). Over the course of about a week the will continue to be fed, their weight will be checked, and there boxes cleaned out to see if the food with 2% JH has any significent effect on the growth of the Tobacco Hornworm. This will also help insure that no outside factors contribute to the results of the experiment. It is important that each hornworm is given the same amount of food so that conditions and doses are identical between all eight worms. There boxes consist of a medium sized square box that is split into four sections. Each section of the box has 2 holes punched into the sides to allow fresh air to cycle in and out of the box. The boxes are placed inside an incubation fridge to maintain a constant ideal growth environment. At the end of the experiment a two sample T test will be run to test if the data is significent to reject the null hypothesis. 



Results:
These results show that the treated group of the tobacco hornworms grew larger than the controll group. Figure 1 shows the average growth of the caterpillars over the course of eleven days. Figure 2 shows the average weight gain of the caterpillars. The second figure shows that the treated group gained on average almost a third more than the controll group. Our data for the two sample T test showed that our p_value was 0.023577. Since this is less than 0.05, our 5 percent, we are able to reject our null hypothesis and we fail to reject our hypothesis.
