Title: Effects of Lidocaine on the Gastrocnemius Muscle of a Frog 

Abstract:
Action potentials orchestrate muscle contractions between neurons and muscle cells. Sodium is a crucial element in action potentials, as they cause the depolarization that begins the action potential. We hypothesize that if we inject the gastrocnemius muscle of a frog with  0.1mL of 25mM of lidocaine, the level of contraction will significantly decrease and the latent period will increase.
 
To test our hypothesis, we directly stimulated the sciatic nerve of a frog leg. We placed the electrodes on the sciatic nerve and watched the gastrocnemius muscle twitch. For each frog leg, we first found the maximum voltage at which the maximum contraction will occur. Then we used that voltage to stimulate the frog legs three times before injecting lidocaine and three times after injecting lidocaine. Each time we measured the amplitude and the length of the latent period. 

As predicted, the length of the latent period increased after injection and the amplitude decreased after injection. This is because the sodium channels were blocked due to lidocaine, a sodium channel blocker. 


Introduction:
About 40% of the human body is made of skeletal muscle. The skeletal muscle is closely integrated with the skeletal system. The neuromuscular junction is the place where the muscle meets the nervous system. Specifically, the neuromuscular junction is a cellular synapse between a motor neuron and a skeletal muscle fiber that enables the translation of chemical cues into physical activity [Vilmont: 2016]. 

At the neuromuscular junction, a nerve stimulates muscle contraction through a process classed excitation-contraction coupling [Johnson: 2018]. This process begins as an action potential travels along an axon of a neuron and reaches the neuromuscular junction. It is difficult to measure the contraction of a muscle fiber, but we can use a myogram to measure contractions at a macroscopic level. There are three main phases in a myogram: the latent period, contraction period and relaxation period [Johnson: 2018]. 

First, an action potential arrives at the post-synaptic terminal of the neuron. The action potential depolarizes the cell and as a result, there is an influx of Na+. Then, Ca2+ enters the cells causing exocytosis of Acetylcholine (ACh). ACh then binds to receptors on the post-synaptic membrane and the action potential beings in the next cell. For this experiment, the post-synaptic cell is a muscle cell, so therefore the action potential causes muscle contraction. 

Lidocaine is a Na+ channel blocker. We predict that if we inject the gastrocnemius muscle with lidocaine, the chemical will cause the sodium channels to be blocked and the amplitude of the contraction will decrease substantially while the latent period increases substantially. 



Materials and Methods:
To test our hypothesis, we preformed direct nerve stimulation to the frog leg. To do this, we had to expose the sciatic nerve. Once we exposed the sciatic nerve, we placed electrodes on the nerve on the two frog legs. Then, for each leg we found the maximum voltage for the maximum contraction to occur. To do this, we directly stimulated the nerve until the muscle made a visible twitch. After the muscle twitched we measured the amplitude of the contraction on iWorx and as long as the amplitude increased at a specific voltage we increased the voltage. Eventually the amplitude stops increasing and that is the maximum voltage for the maximum contraction. 

After we determined the voltage for maximum contraction, we stimulated the nerve at that voltage three times and measured the latent period for both frog legs. Then, we injected the gastrocnemius muscle of both frog legs with 0.1mL of 25mM of lidocaine. We preformed the same measurements ad we did before we injected the frog leg. Lastly, we compared the amplitude (level of contraction) and latent period of the myogram before we injected the frog legs with lidocaine and after we injected the frog legs with lidocaine. 


Results:
Figure 1 suggests that on average, that the amplitude of the contraction was larger before the injection of lidocaine. The average difference in the amplitude (before treatment - after treatment) is -0.237. The mean amplitude of the contraction after the lidocaine injection was significantly smaller than the mean amplitude before the lidocaine injection (t-stat = 12.787, df = 1, P = 0.0497). In comparison, Figure 2 suggests that the length of the latent period after the lidocaine injection is longer. Unlike the results for amplitude, the mean length of the latent period after injection is not statistically larger than the mean length of the latent period before the lidocaine injection (t-stat = 3, df = 1, P = 0.205). This is because the p-value for the mean difference in amplitude before and after treatment is smaller than 0.05 but the p-value for the mean different in length of latent period is not smaller than 0.05.
